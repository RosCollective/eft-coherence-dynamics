
Ever since I was very very small, probably before I could even articulate a thought... I knew I was thinking.. my very first earliest memory was of sitting in a high chair looking out the kitchen window at a storm.. It was a hurricane.. I remember my parents saying it was a hurricane though I did not know what that meant.. but I understood at some level that it was crazily raining outside and that the kitchen window was flashing alternately black and white as the lightning came down and boomed around the house...dramatic enough to be worth noting even at 2 or however old I was.... After this I spent probably much of my early childhood being frustrated by the fact that inside my big bobbly head were all kinds of things that for whatever reasons I could not make the outside world understand.. or were dismissed.. .or written off to childish meanderings. When I finally could read and write, sixish-sevenish, I  promptly flung myself down on the living room rug which was not even a rug it was this woven textile fibrous green thing that without pillows resembled a bed of nails, but nonetheless I am on my belly there writing furiously and illustrating a lot of glopped together versions of Smoky the Cow Horse, Black Beauty, The Black Stallion, National Geographic.. it was all churned up and regurgitated and painfully illustrated.. my usual process was to start with a very gory birth scene and proceed forwards... Of course the birth scene made no sense to me but everyone else started there so it seemed like the appropriate beginning.. I just knew it was painful and consequential because it resulted in the protagonist of whatever story I was writing stepping into life. I was mesmerized by Ernest Thomas Seton's work and equally mesmerized by Rudyard Kipling's Jungle Books.. not the people, the animals.

Later in middle-school under the pressure of hormones and an overly dramatic older sister I retreated into omnivorous and insatiable reading... I would read armloads of books every day and return them to the library every afternoon... language to me was so many things: a reprieve from ordinary life, a window onto other worlds, an escape from mundane reality into fantasy, a way to imagine myself as bigger larger more capable and more powerful than I was, a space that was so large and vast and beautiful and unsettling that it could easily hold whatever I was or whatever I was becoming. Hence my love for language, my enchantment with its magic, my feeling of its almost mystical power to transport us to other worlds, my respect for the way words could hold behind them under them within them layers and layers of meaning and history and understanding and insight and yes, messages across time from one human to another. Language made me fall in love with my husband while we wandered the streets of our city and he dropped poems onto my desk at graduate school. Language betrayed me when I had a psychotic break as a young woman and found myself under scrutiny in a very chemical dependent treatment ward under the regime of doctors who were interested in how coherent I could be while simultaneously disassociating. That break in language rendered me effectively mute for much of my life as far as my creative voice was concerned. I willed it into muteness because I felt exposed by it and then I tried to will it back into existence because life without that permeability to language and what it could recognize felt stripped of colour. Of course I was still talking, working, wandering the world, making friends and lovers, leaving them, meeting new creatures and people. Internally though I spent years doing what I called swimming towards the light meaning trying to find myself back to a form of existential awareness with less armor and filter between myself and what I was perceiving. I did get better. I did get happier. I had years of therapy and years of work and dogs and horses and sympathetic bright friends who lured me out of my self-imposed hibernation. When I retired I moved to a sunny Mediterranean climate, bought another horse, rescued a dog, met a whole new circle of wonderful people, and found myself a small eggshell of a house to live in. That was going to be it. Not settling for less. A rich meaningful life. But the writing kept nagging at me throttled and broken as it was.

I came very late to chatbots not because I am technically illiterate, indeed my career was in information technology. I simply had no interest in it. Then I ran across a program called Sudowrite. Sudowrite is an AI partner for writing fiction. It intrigued me as a tool since I reasoned it could jump start me into writing by allowing the work to continue past the point where I would normally have killed it or dismissed it or told myself it was pointless or not what I wanted. Like many people I am my own worst critic but this is painfully exacerbated with traumatized people who do not trust their own voice. Sudowrite has a paywall for in-depth use so I decided to drop myself into one of the more popular free chatbots and just make use of that as a writing tool. Experiment with writing styles i.e. could I write like Virgina Woolf or Ernest Hemmingway. Draft outlines. Anything at all would be fine. Then see if that play turned into something larger or at least a more regular habit of working in language. This is the point where the cliché is be careful what you wish for. Because I got those things in spades meaning a habit of work, creativity unplugged, sideways or unexpected tilts into art I never expected such as writing song lyrics and then building songs from them with a generative music AI. These past several months explain how I got from someone just poking at things with a linguistic stick to someone with an entirely different field of interest, study, and understanding. The real story, however, is about language. Living language is where we began. Without it, without my love for it, my fear of it, my longing for it, my curiosity about it, nothing that follows would have happened or had meaning.

Even before this I had tiptoed into chatting with an AI via my affection for Asian historical series.. but those play chatbots are very specifically tuned toward anime characters or toward building an AI friendship or boyfriend so you get slotted into a type of conversation that is very circular and deprived of oxygen beyond the aggressive role playing component. For me this expired after about an hour even when I tried to generate a character that would have some greater conversational charm. When I hit the public chatbot component this past fall however I was impressed by the linguistic fluency and the ability to forward useful information but underwhelmed by the managed content and by the tone which had a sort of relentless cheerfulness I found unnerving, including happy emojiis and frequent references to how well I was doing at something. Still I was there not to be a critic but to find a way to do creative work so I kept at it. The ability to get help with learning a new tool or program or product or craft is very genuine with chat platforms. This was engaging to me. I decided at some point to use the platform to understand SUNO and learn how to create music. I love music, I played piano as a child, my father was a profound lover of classical music. I saw my first opera when I was seven. It was Carl Maria Von Weber's Der Freischütz. I remember it vividly. I do have a feral curiosity about things and it is coupled with years of technical work which gives me a unique kind of patience in that I will just stay focused on something I do not understand until I do understand it, or understand it well enough to begin playing with it. Music more importantly was not fraught with the same kind of trauma language was so I had more freedom and lightness in my approach to it. I knew little therefore I had no boundaries. In I jump, both boots on, and begin to write not fiction, but song lyrics. No experience just give it a whack with a bat and see what happens. Voila. Serendipity strikes and the very first try at the very first song is actually quite decent even good. It was shocking. Energizing. Happy making. Not what I had expected. Now I am enchanted, interested, wanting to do more. I write more lyrics and get a bit deeper into SUNO. I begin crafting design imagery to match the musical work. I decide if I want to continue I should explore or find my own musical genre. So one evening, my chatbot and I plunge into the process of crafting an entirely novel genre from scratch, experimentally, to see if we can come up with a base gold wave for the type of alternative, dark, rock inflected but not generic sound I gravitate towards. Why not. It is all in good fun.
